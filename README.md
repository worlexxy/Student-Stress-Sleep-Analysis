# Student-Stress-Sleep-Analysis
This project focuses on analyzing the relationship between academic stress and sleep patterns among students, providing insights into how these factors interact across different demographic groups. Understanding this relationship is essential for improving both academic performance and overall student health.

Microsoft Excel was used as the primary analytical tool, employing techniques such as data cleaning, pivot tables, and data visualization (charts and graphs) to explore trends and relationships within the data. These methodologies enable efficient summarization and comparison of key variables, forming the foundation for the insights presented in this report.

DATA SPLITTING AND PROCESSING

PROJECT SPLITS: 

Independent Value: Age Group, Gender, Current Education Level

Dependent Value: How much academic pressure do you feel?
How often do you feel stressed due to studies?
How many hours do you sleep on average per night?

INDUSTRY TYPE: Education Industry

STORY OF DATA

This dataset represents responses collected from students across different age groups, genders, and education levels to understand their academic stress, pressure, and sleep patterns. The data captures how students perceive academic pressure, how frequently they feel stressed due to studies, and how many hours they sleep on average per night.
By combining demographic information (such as age group and gender) with academic and lifestyle factors (stress levels and sleep duration), the dataset helps reveal patterns in student well-being. It allows us to explore whether stress levels differ across age groups, genders, and education stages, and how academic stress may be associated with reduced sleep.

DATA CLEANING

To ensure accuracy and reliability, several data cleaning steps were applied:
* Duplicate Removal: Records with identical student attributes (age, gender, education level, stress, sleep hours) were identified and removed to prevent bias.
* Data Type Consistency: Converted numerical fields such as stress levels and sleep hours into proper numeric formats. Ensured categorical variables (age group, gender, education level) were consistently labeled.

VISUALIZATION PREPARATION: Structured data for charts (bar graphs, scatter plots, trend lines) to highlight relationships between variables.


STAKEHOLDERS OF THE PROJECT

The key stakeholders include students, educational institutions, faculty members, counselors, parents, and education policymakers who can use the insights to improve student well being and academic outcomes.

WHAT SUCCESS MEANS TO THE INDUSTRY

In the education industry, success is not measured only by academic performance, but also by the overall well being of students. For this project, success means creating an environment where students can achieve academic goals without excessive stress and maintain healthy sleep patterns.

IN-ANALYSIS

-Academic Stress Levels Across Education

Observation: College students show the highest stress, followed by university students, while other education groups have the lowest stress.

Insight (Growth): As students progress academically, pressure increases indicating a need to scale support systems alongside academic advancement to sustain performance and well being.

-Academic Pressure vs Sleep Hours

Observation: There is a clear downward trend higher academic pressure leads to fewer hours of sleep.

Insight (Growth): Rising academic demands can negatively impact health and productivity, so managing pressure effectively can improve both student performance and long term outcomes.

-Average Sleep by Education Level

Observation: University and other groups average around 8 hours of sleep, while college students sleep less (~7.6 hrs).

Insight (Growth): Reduced sleep among college students suggests a risk of burnout, meaning improving time management and workload balance could enhance academic efficiency and retention.

-Academic Pressure & Sleep by Age Group

Observation: Younger students (Under 15) have high stress, while older groups show lower stress and more stable sleep patterns.

Insight (Growth): Stress management skills appear to improve with age, highlighting the importance of early intervention programs to build resilience sooner.

-Average Sleep Duration by Age & Gender

Observation: Females aged 15–18 get the least sleep, while most male groups and older students average around 8 hours.

Insight (Growth):
 There is a gender and age specific gap, suggesting targeted support for teenage girls could significantly improve health, focus, and academic outcomes

POST ANALYSIS

OBSERVATIONS

* Academic pressure and sleep show a clear inverse relationship as stress increases, sleep decreases.
* Under 15 students experience the highest stress, which is unusual for that age group.
* College students have the highest stress and lower sleep, making them a high risk group.
* Female students aged 15–18 get the least sleep, indicating a gender-specific gap.
* University and older age groups (23+) maintain better sleep (~8 hrs) and more stable stress levels.
* Stress levels tend to decrease with age, suggesting improved coping mechanisms over time.
* Overall sleep average looks healthy, but specific groups are underperforming, especially teens and college students.

RECOMMENDATION

-Reduce early academic pressure (Under 15): Introduce balanced curricula, reduce excessive competition, and promote activity based learning.

-Support college students: Provide mental health resources, academic counseling, and time management training to reduce burnout.

-Improve sleep awareness: Educate students on sleep hygiene (consistent schedules, limiting screen time, proper rest habits).

-Focus on female students (15–18): Offer targeted interventions like counseling, stress management programs, and wellness support.
Balance academic workload: Avoid excessive assignments and tight deadlines; encourage breaks and extracurricular activities.

-Promote stress management programs: Include mindfulness, relaxation techniques, and physical activities in student routines.

-Continuous monitoring: Regularly track stress and sleep data to identify at risk groups early and take action.

CONCLUSION

This project shows a clear relationship between academic pressure, stress, and sleep among students. As academic pressure increases, students tend to feel more stressed and sleep for fewer hours, which can negatively affect their health and academic performance.

The analysis also identifies certain high risk groups. Students under 15 experience unexpectedly high levels of stress at an early stage. College students face the highest academic pressure and also get less sleep, making them more vulnerable to burnout. In addition, female students aged 15–18 get the least sleep, highlighting a gender specific concern.

The data further shows that stress levels decrease with age, suggesting that students develop better coping mechanisms over time. However, this also indicates the need for early support, as younger students may struggle more with managing pressure.

Overall, the findings emphasize that success in education should not be based only on academic performance but also on student well being. Maintaining a balance between studies, mental health, and proper sleep is essential. By reducing academic pressure and promoting healthy habits, educational institutions and stakeholders can help students perform better while also improving their overall quality of life.
